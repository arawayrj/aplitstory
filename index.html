Into the Storm

By: RJ, Alan, & Justin

The boy, whose name is Santiago, stood facing death as he tried desperately to turn himself into the wind. Considering the words of the Alchemist, he reached out to the spirits of the world. He tried first with the desert, but alas, the desert could not turn him into the wind despite its willingness. It did, however, let him know that he should talk to the wind. The boy listened and tried his luck with the wind. Santiago managed to annoy the wind to the point where it picked up considerably. Eventually, a sandstorm was created so the boy could speak to the sun without going blind. The sun also was unable to transform Santiago into the wind, so it suggested that he speak to the Hand That Wrote All. Santiago prayed instead of speaking, and eventually saw that the soul of the Hand That Wrote All was within himself. He managed to disappear then reappear on the opposite side of the camp he was being held at, thus securing his life as well as the Alchemist’s.

Santiago was not always the pseudo-alchemist that he became when he turned himself into the wind. He began his journey as a simple shepherd in the fields of Andalusia. This region of Spain was regarded as one of the most beautiful places around the land. Santiago’s father was puzzled when Santiago asked to leave, because he know that people come from all around the world to live where they had lived. The women were more beautiful and the crops were more full. He starts out as a boy who just follows his sheep, a passive observer of life. This contrasts with his eventual end state of being in complete control of himself, but more on that later. Somewhat content with life, Santiago began having a dream that would return each night he slept under a specific tree that had sprouted up from the ruins of a church. It fed into his hunger for exploration that he had harbored for some time. In the dream, he was told about a treasure located in egypt, and he was told that it was his to find. Santiago desperately wanted to make sense of this dream. Eventually, Santiago decided to ask a gypsy to interpret his dream, but seemed dissatisfied with the results, as she basically told him he must travel to the pyramids, which was something he probably could have guessed himself.

Santiago’s treasure that he was headed towards was revealed to be his Personal Legend by someone called Melchizedek, who was one of the pieces along Santiago’s journey that guided him by offering insight as to how Santiago should act. In the city of Tarifa, Santiago met the mysterious being who called himself Melchizedek, the King of Salem. At first, Santiago was understandably wary of this man who claimed to be a king. Initially annoyed at the old man for pestering him with questions about his book, the man proved to the boy that he could read, and in fact had read the very book the boy was holding in his hands. As the boy looked down on those who couldn’t read, by striking up a conversation about the book Santiago had with him, Melchizedek was able to convince Santiago to take him seriously, since an old man who could read was more likely a king than an old man who couldn’t. Eventually, Melchizedek informed Santiago of the importance of a concept known as the Personal Legend. Santiago seemed to enjoy the idea of a personal legend, since it gave meaning to his life - although Santiago enjoyed traveling with his sheep, he believed what would make him truly happy would be finding some greater truth or meaning. The king explains that the Personal Legend is essentially someone’s ultimate dream or goal, something that every person innately wants to accomplish. Over time, however, people become less and less aware of what their personal legend is, for an unknown reason. As Melchizedek puts it, everyone, when they are young, knows what their Personal Legend is. At that point in their lives, everything is clear and everything is possible. They are not afraid to dream, and to yearn for everything they would like to see happen to them in their lives. But, as time passes, a mysterious force begins to convince them that it would be impossible for them to realize their Personal Legend. Melchizedek elaborated on how the key to pursuing one’s Personal Legend is to follow one’s heart, as he explained to Santiago, and to ignore the outside influences that may try to change him. As an example, he brought up the baker across the street, who ended up abandoning his Personal Legend of traveling because he believed a baker was a more important profession. Santiago considered this and the thought of remaining a shepherd, compared with being able to travel freely like the wind, and thought about buying a ticket aboard a boat to Africa. Melchizedek was able to finally convince Santiago with a story of a boy who was sent to learn from the wisest man in the world, whom the boy finds inside a beautiful castle. The wise man tells the boy to carry a spoon of oil through the castle, and not to spill a drop. The boy succeeds in the task, but when he tells the man that he did not see a single thing in the wonderful castle, he is made to repeat the task. This time, he sees many amazing things, but has spilled all the oil in the spoon. Santiago understood this to mean that while he should go out to look at the world, he must never forget the things close to him, just as a shepherd may travel but must never forget his flock.

... whoever has money is never really alone.

After arriving in Tangier, Morocco, Santiago is robbed for all the money he earned by selling his sheep. Although he is initially distraught by this setback, he is reassured and encouraged by the king’s words: when you want something, all the universe conspires in helping you to achieve it. Now more determined than ever, Santiago happens upon a crystal shop. The shop owner offers Santiago a job working for him in order to make money for his trip to Egypt, as he considered Santiago a good omen. Throughout their year of working, Santiago and the crystal merchant had profound effects on each other. Santiago was able to greatly increase business, by first building a display case to put outside the store in order to attract more customers, and then coming up with the idea of selling tea within crystal glasses. In both cases, he was met with reluctance from the crystal merchant, who did not understand why one would undertake risk for uncertain reward, especially when things are already good enough. This is the same philosophy that the crystal merchant uses when it comes to his life. Unlike Santiago, who wanted to pursue his personal legend so he can be happy, the crystal merchant was unwilling to pursue his own dreams for fear that once he succeeds, he will have nothing left to live for. Indeed, he believed that he would be happiest among what he is most familiar with, which is selling crystal. At that point, he thought, there was no point in travelling to Mecca, where his Personal Legend was leading him him, as he may as well live out the rest of his life the way he has been living it. Soon after Santiago leaves the crystal merchant for Egypt, the crystal merchant became rather upset. He reflected on how Santiago had been able to quickly turn his business around by taking a few risks, and he thinks of how much happier he could have been had he stepped outside his comfort zone and went to pursue his Personal Legend. Santiago, on the other hand, recognizes the crystal merchant as an example of why one experience new things in order to grow. He reflects on this fact when he considers his sheep - although his sheep are able to teach Santiago a great many things, he has learned just as much in his time away from them, things that his sheep could never have taught him. Armed with this conclusion, is more convinced that his choice in pursuing his Personal Legend is something that he must do.

While in Al-Fayoum, Santiago met a woman named Fatima, and fell in love at first sight. Santiago described it as: "At that moment, it seemed to him that time stood still, and the Soul of the World surged within him. When he looked into her dark eyes, and saw that her lips were poised between a laugh and silence, he learned the most important part of the language that all the world spoke - the language that everyone on earth was capable of understanding in their heart. It was love." Fatima played an important part in Santiago’s journey, as another guiding figure, and like the other people that Santiago met on his journey, she is also is his motivation. Santiago wanted to give everything up and live with her forever, but she said that he must pursue his dream, his Personal Legend. She described their love as the desert: the dunes are changed by the wind, but the desert never changes. She also tells him: if I am truly a part of your dream, then you will come back. Santiago loved her, and respected her when she pushed him to pursue his Legend. From their love, Santiago learns more about the truth of the Soul of the World, a concept that unites all entities, as everything comes from the same thing. Santiago knows that the mutual love between him and Fatima exists because they are able to communicate through the Language of the World, the other link between all entities, and through this he is able to trust that he will return to Fatima one day, and is one step closer to knowing what being an alchemist is all about.

I am a desert woman, but most importantly, I am a woman

When Santiago arrives at Al-Fayoum, the desert oasis the caravan stops at to rest, he stumbles across a very wise and powerful man called the Alchemist, who adopts Santiago as his apprentice. Santiago found he needed help from somebody who possessed the skills he desired, and the Alchemist was just the person. Luckily for Santiago, the Alchemist was trying to pursue his own Legend, in part of which he had to take on an apprentice. The Alchemist taught Santiago survival skills in the desert, as well as teaching him to turn lead into gold, but most importantly, he taught Santiago to read omens. Through this skill, Santiago learns to understand the world and how it works, and to be able to use this knowledge to his own benefit. He learns of something called the Language of the World, an unspoken language that everything that exists is able to understand. This is how he was able to understand omens that guided him the rest of the way to his Personal Legend. It is only after meeting the Alchemist that Santiago truly realizes what the concepts of alchemy are, that everything is connected to one another, and to understand all the connections and to be able to manipulate them is how one is able to fully understand the world.

The people that Santiago met guided him through the adversity that he faced on his journey. Each person offered the solutions to tests that Santiago had to learn how to answer. The universe put these people in front of him so he would learn how to fulfil his legend. The sandstorm was his final test of adversity, and the universe had recognized and respected his struggle in it. Santiago was able to learn from his struggle, and when he was facing death, the universe had conspired to make sure he succeeded in his task. Through Santiago’s perseverance, he was able to make his legend true, find his treasure, and get back to his love, Fatima. Just like Santiago’s journey, life presents us with a series of tests that we all must overcome to reach our goals, whatever they may be. Life may seem like a desert - harsh and forbidding, but we must look into it to find its beauty and learn all that it has to teach us. Like with Santiago, we must choose to live our lives according to what we believe is right, and not let others keep us from our dreams. We must look past what can be seen merely on the surface, and once we do that, we can understand both ourselves and the world.
